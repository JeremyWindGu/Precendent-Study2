1. Ontological Analysis

This section outlines what exists in the game and its core components:

  • Player: A political decision-maker responsible for steering the planet through crisis.
  • Policy Options: Dietary shifts (e.g., veganism), energy transitions (solar, nuclear, CCS), land usage (Half-Earth, agroecology), geoengineering options.
  • Timeline: The game progresses in 5-year increments, with each turn evaluating the impact of the chosen strategy.

  • Geospatial Grid: A divided world map representing ecological and social variation across global regions.
  • Simulation Engine: A global system modeling feedback between CO₂, biodiversity, food, energy, and social indicators.
  • Feedback System: Visual response in maps, emissions curves, biodiversity indicators, and narrative summaries.

2. Historical & Contextual Analysis

The game was developed in a specific political, ecological, and technological moment. It is a response to:

  • Disillusionment post-COP26: Highlighting the failure of incrementalist green policy.
  • Degrowth and Eco-Socialism Movements: Offering a vision aligned with radical ecological thought.
  • Digital Media Shift: Leveraging the game medium to explore complex, interconnected planetary issues.

The authors envisioned the game as a pedagogical and speculative tool — a way to force players into confronting real trade-offs and ideological commitments rather than techno-solutionist illusions.

3. Visual & Aesthetic Analysis

The game’s aesthetics serve its ideology. It avoids realistic or immersive environments and instead deploys a visual language of planning and abstraction:

  • Retro Data Graphics: Echoing Cold War-era scientific utopianism.
  • Flat Functional Interfaces: Rejects gamified spectacle in favor of decision literacy.
  • World Map Logic: Grid-based geography and planetary scale frame decision-making beyond nation-states.
